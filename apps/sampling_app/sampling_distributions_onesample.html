Statistical errors and power

All of the sample mean temperatures we've generated have come from a population with a mean of 37℃, and all of the variation is just random sampling. But, when we get sample means that occur by chance less than 5% of the time we reject the null, and conclude that body temperature is not the null value of 37℃. This is an error when the null is actually true, and it happens 5% of the time when we test with an α level of 0.05. This is the false positive, or Type I error, and we choose how big it is when we pick the α level.

When the null hypothesis is actually false, and the mean is something other than exactly 37℃, then getting a p-value over 0.05 causes us to retain the null in error. This is the false negative, or Type II error, or β. We don't pick the value of β, but we have some control over how big it is likely to be.

This app shows the relationship between the status of the null hypothesis (whether it is true or false in reality) and statistical errors. It also allows you to change the sample size so that you can see how sample size affects error rates (or not!). Finally, you can switch between the x-axis displaying t-values, or displaying body temperatures in ℃ (that is, the data units) so that you can explore the process from either perspective.
