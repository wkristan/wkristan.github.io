Using the t-distribution

The t-distribution can be used as a model of random sampling. Its shape depends on degrees of freedom. The x-axis is standard errors, and the number of standard errors to encompass 95% of the curve around the middle is called the critical value.

Statistical errors and power

When you conduct a null hypothesis significance test you draw a definite conclusion (reject the null, or retain the null) by setting a cutoff point for considering differences to be real or not. But, drawing the line at p < 0.05 is a little arbitrary - the curves are smooth, they do not have any noticeable break or change in shape at the point that we draw this line. Deciding that an experiment that gives us a p-value of 0.049 indicates a real effect of the treatment, while one with a p-value of 0.051 is just random sampling is hard to justify.

This app shows the relationship between the status of the null hypothesis (whether it is true or false in reality) and statistical errors. It also allows you to change the sample size so that you can see how sample size affects error rates (or not!). Finally, you can switch between the x-axis displaying t-values, or displaying differences in yield (that is, the data units) so that you can explore the process from either perspective.

If the null is false, only Type II (β) error is possible. We can only measure β error for a specific alternative. In reality we don't know what the alternative is, but setting the amount of difference between the population means shows how β and power are affected by the size of difference, the alpha level, and the sample size.
