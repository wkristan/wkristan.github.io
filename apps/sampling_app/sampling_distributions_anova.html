What to expect from random sampling

The great challenge we have in interpreting experimental results is that random variation will produce differences between sample means. This is true even if we apply no treatment at all, and just take several random samples from the same population and compare their means.

If a treatment is having no effect, then for all practical purposes the results of the experiment will be no different from taking random samples from the same distribution. There will no doubt be differences between the sample means, because random samples almost never produce identical means, but the differences are just random sampling effects if the treatment doesn't do anything.

We can never be 100% certain that an observed difference isn't due to random sampling. But, we can minimize the chances that we'll end up treating a random difference as a real one if we know how much variation is produced by random chance alone. Then, if our observed results look very different from what chance usually produces we can conclude they are probably a real effect of treatment. If instead the means are no more different than we expect to get by chance we should conclude that the difference is only due to random chance.

To see how this works with ANOVA, the app below selects three samples from the same normal distribution. It calls one of the groups "Control", one "Treatment 1", and the third "Treatment 2", but these are just labels to mimic what you would do in an actual experiment. In reality, there is no difference between the groups at the population level, because all three are randomly sampled from the same population.

Each time a sample is selected, the ANOVA is conducted and an F-value is computed. The graph for a single experiment is on the left, and the F-value obtained from it is added to the graph on the right.
