Two-tailed t-tests detect any difference from μ. A x̄ that is either above or below μ could be statistically significant.

One-tailed t-tests only detect differences in the direction selected, either "Greater than μ" or "Less than μ".

We are reporting the test results as a p-value - the test is statistically significant if p is less than the alpha level you've selected, 0.05.

The p-value is calculated as the area under the t-distribution from tobs to the tail indicated as the alternative - with 'Greater than μ' selected the curve is shaded from tobs to positive infinity.

We are reporting the test results using a rejection and retention region. With a two tailed test selected 2.5% of the curve is placed in each of the tails, and we reject the null if the observed t-value falls into this rejection region.

Size of difference that is detectable

To begin there is no difference to detect - the sample mean and population mean are the same.

Set the display to p-value, and increase the value of x̄ to 37.3. This makes x̄ - μ equal to 0.3, which increases the observed t-value to 1.355. The p-value for an observed t-value is 0.2084.

Change the display to reject/retain regions, then increase the sample mean until it just touches the reject region at 37.51 (the tails turn blue). The observed t-value increases to 2.304, which has a p-value of 0.0467 - this is finally statistically significant.

Switch to a one-tailed test, with an alternative of greater than μ - you can now decrease the sample mean to 37.41 and it will still be statistically significant - a smaller difference is detectable with a one-tailed test, if it's in the expected direction.

If you switch to the alternative with mean less than μ you'll see the difference is no longer signficant because it went in the opposite direction that is being tested for.

Effect of sample size

Set the sample mean to 37.1, set the Tails to Two, and show the results as Reject/retain region. You'll see that the observed t-value is 0.452, which is solidly inside of the retention region - with this sample size a difference of 0.1 degrees could easily be random sampling variation.

Now start increasing the sample size and watch as the observed t-value gets bigger. Increasing n isn't changing the sample mean, but it makes the standard error smaller - for the same amount of difference we gain more confidence that it's real as we collect more data.

Continue to increase the sample size until the observed t-value enters the rejection region in the upper tail - you'll see that when n reaches 191 the difference of 0.1 degrees becomes statstically significant. Small differences become detectable when we have a lot of data.

Degrees of freedom also go up as n increases, which narrows the t-distribution - smaller t-values are needed to fall into the rejection region if the t-distribution is narrow.

Increasing n always increases the chances of detecting a real difference for a two-tailed test, but only does so for a one-tailed test if the difference is in the direction being tested for. Switch to a one-tailed test and set the alternative to less than μ, and you'll see that increasing sample size makes the observed t move away from the rejection region.

One-tailed t-tests

Select the one-tailed t-test option now.

Show the results using the "Reject/retain regions" option, and with the alternative set to Greater than μ you'll see that only an increase in temperature can fall into a rejection region with this choice of one-tailed test.

If you change the alternative to Less than μ the rejection region is placed in the lower tail, and only a decrease in temperature could ever be statistically significant with that choice of one-tailed test.

Note though that the rejection region in the single tail that has one is greater than with a two-tailed test. All of the alpha level's probability is placed in one tail in a one-tailed test, but it is split between both tails for a two-tailed test.

One-tailed testing thus can detect a smaller change than a two-tailed test can, provided it's in the right direction.

Two-tailed t-tests

Two-tailed tests can detect differences in either direction. It is rare that a significant difference in an unexpected direction would be uninteresting, so two-tailed testing is much more common than one-tailed testing.

The p-value in a two-tailed test is obtained by calculating an observed t-value and using it (and its negative) to find the area under the "tails" of the curve from that point to infinity. These tails represent the random outcomes that would be as far from μ or further than what we observed. If most random differences are smaller than what we observed then the probability will be small, but if most random differences are as big or bigger than observed the probability will be large.

Initially the sample mean is set to equal the null hypothesis of 37, so the t-value is 0, and all of the curve is shaded, giving us a p-value of 1.

If you switch Show results using to "Reject/retain regions" you'll see that an observed t-value that falls far enough from μ in either direction will end up in a rejection region. When the observed t-value is big enough to fall in a rejection region it will also have a p-value that is less than the alpha level we've selected, and thus viewed from either perspective it will be statsitically significant (how much of the tails is shaded is dictated by α, so the p-value method and reject/retain region method are two sides of the same coin).

Change the alpha level

Alpha is the cutoff value we use to decide if a p-value is significant, and to set the sizes of the rejection regions on the t-distribution.

Set the display to reject/retain regions, and then increase the alpha level - you'll see that the rejection region increases in size.

We're able to detect smaller differences if we set alpha to a larger value, but this comes at a price - the alpha level is also the probability of makeing a Type I, or false positive, error. If the null hypothesis is true 5% of the random samples we draw from a population with a mean of 37 will fall into the rejection regions and cause us to reject the null mistakenly when it's true.

So, it is possible to detect smaller differences by increasing alpha, but it also increases our false positive error rate. If we want to detect smaller differences it's better to increase sample size, because doing so doesn't increase our false positive rate.

Change the standard deviation

If you decrease the standard deviation the observed t-value will increase (assuming the sample mean is something other than 37) because smaller standard deviations result in smaller standard errors. We have a better ability to detect small differences if the standard deviation is small.

The standard deviation is largely a function of the biological organisms we're working with, and we may not be able to control its size the way we can control other aspects of the study. But, we can minimize s by being careful in our data collection, by using homogeneous subjects, or by accounting for any differences that cause variation in the data we're aware of in our analysis.
