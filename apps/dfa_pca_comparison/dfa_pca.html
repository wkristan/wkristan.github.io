DFA and PCA

There are some clear similarities between DFA and PCA, so much so that sometimes researchers will conduct PCA and then use PCA scores in an ANOVA to compare groups. If you want to compare groups, though, a DFA is a better choice - click on the LD1, PC1 and PC2 entries in the legend for the scatter plot on the left to see why this is the case.

With two groups the LD1 goes through the group centroids, and it is all that is needed to separate the groups as well as possible. Points projected perpendicularly to the LD1 axis for the calico (red dots) and white (black dots) clam shells are non-overlapping. Consequently, the red and black lines do not overlap along the LD1 axis of the parallel coordinates plot.

The first PCA axis is positioned to maximally account for variation in the data, without reference to the species groupings. There is a fairly strong correlation between minor axis and width, which causes more variation in the data than the difference between group centroids done. Because of this PC1 falls along the axis of the correlation between the variables, but does not separate the groups well. Points for each shell type projected to PC1 overlap in the middle in the parallel coordinates plot, making the differences between the species seem less clear when using PC1 than when using LD1.

The second PCA axis is positioned to account for variation in the data independent of the first. While this seems to align PC2 similarly to LD1 in this example, when the points are projected to PC2 the species still overlap in the middle, and make the shells seem less distinctly different than they are.
