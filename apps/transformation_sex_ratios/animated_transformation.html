Linear scale (no transformation) Log scale


Counts of males or females are bell shaped (graphs on the right), but ratios of m/f are skewed (upper left), which is clear on the normal probability plot (lower left). The arrow indicating equal numbers of males and females (m = f) is at 1 on a linear scale, which is not in the middle of the data - values extend to nearly 3 above this point, but can only have a minimum of 0. A sex ratio of 3 means that there are 3 times as many males as females, and a sex ratio of 1/3 (or 0.333) means that there are three times as many females as males, but even though these are equally different from even numbers 3 is much further away on a linear scale from 1 than 0.333 is.

If you drag the slider to the right they transition into log scale. You will see that:

  • Any number raised to the power of 0 is 1, so the location along the x-axis on a log scale where m = f is at 0
  • The log ratios are symmetrical around 0. This is because the natural log of 3 is 1.1, and the natural log of 1/3 is -1.1 - so, on a log scale these are the same distance away from 0, but a female-biased ratio is negative and a male-biased ratio is positive.

Using a log-transformation on these data set the mean to 0,  and made deviations away from equal number symmetrical around that mean - thus, the data became normally distributed, which you can see in the histogram of log ratios, and the normal probability plot of the transformed data.
